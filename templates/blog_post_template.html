How to Write a Letter of Inquiry that Gets Noticed

By Lucia Ballesteros · June 28, 2025 · 7 min read

Key Takeaways

  • Focus on alignment, not just need. Show the funder why your mission aligns with *their* mission.
  • Use data from past giving to inform your "ask amount" and project description.
  • Keep it concise. An LOI is a summary, not a full proposal. Aim for two pages max.

Your Letter of Inquiry (LOI) is your organization's first impression. In a world where foundation program officers are inundated with requests, a generic, boilerplate letter is a one-way ticket to the "no" pile. So how do you break through the noise? It's not about shouting louder; it's about speaking their language.

The secret is to shift your mindset from "we need funding" to "we are the perfect partner to help you achieve your goals." This guide will walk you through the essential steps to craft an LOI that stands out and gets you to the next stage: the full proposal.

Step 1: Master the Art of a Compelling Summary

The opening paragraph is the most critical part of your entire letter. You must summarize your project, the need, and the alignment with the funder in three to four sentences. Do not bury the lead. Be direct, confident, and clear.

"We've found that foundations are 70% more likely to respond positively to an LOI that states the exact ask amount and project alignment in the first paragraph."

Step 2: Use Data to Justify, Not Just Describe

Instead of simply stating that a problem exists, use data to illustrate the scope of the problem and the impact of your proposed solution. This demonstrates that your approach is evidence-based and that you are a sophisticated, professional organization.

Step 3: The Budget - Be Transparent and Realistic

Provide a clear, high-level summary of your project budget. You don't need to provide a line-item breakdown in the LOI, but you do need to show that you have a realistic understanding of the costs involved. Clearly state the total project budget and the specific amount you are requesting from the foundation.

This transparency builds trust and shows that you are a responsible steward of funds.
